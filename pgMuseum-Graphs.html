Alkalinity

Alkalinity is a test that looks specifically at the amount of carbonate in water, which makes water more basic. This is important because most potential sources of pollution will make water more acidic. Measuring the natural level of alkalinity in water helps us to know how well it can tolerate added substances that might change its pH (a quality known in chemistry as buffering capacity). An alkanity result of 50 ppm or higher indicates a high buffering capacity.

PH

The pH test measures how acidic or alkaline (basic) water is. Natural sources of acidic include decaying organic matter and natural sources of alkalinity include minerals like limestone. Water can become more acidic due to a variety of types of waterwater runoff and other sources of pollution. Steelhead are very sensitive to pH. They thrive in a range of 6.5 to 8.5. A pH of lower than 6 or higher than 9 is stressful and a ph of lower than 5 or higher than 10 is lethal.

Flow Velocity

When water levels are low this can often mean lower rates of flow. Flow is a measure of how fast water is moving. Steelhead are unlikely to swim upstream to spawn if the flow at a river mouth is less than 1.2 meters per second. Juvenile and adult steelhead require a minimum flow velocity of .3 to .6 meters per second to thrive. Higher flow also creates turbulence, which brings oxygen into the water.

Turbidity

Turbidity is a measure of how clear water is, which indicates how much solid material is in the water. Turbidity can increase due to erosion, buildup of ash from wildfires, and sediment buildup due to dams. These factors are also often related to habitat loss. A result of 7 Jackson Turbidity Units (JTUs) or lower indicates high water quality. A result of 23 JTUs or higher is poor.

Salinity

Salinity is a measure of how much salt is in water. Even inland water sources can become saline if saltwater intrusion occurs. Saltwater intrusion happens when groundwater resources are overdrawn along coastal areas, causing seawater to enter aquifers. We test salinity to check for saltwater intrusion and to check the quality of the lagoon habitat at Carmel River State Beach, where salinity fluctuates with access to the ocean throughout the year. Juvenile steelhead require water near 0 parts per thousand (ppt) salt to survive, but when steelhead undergo smoltification- a biological process that prepares them for life in the ocean- .5 to 30 ppt is ideal.

Air Temperature

High temperature, and the low DO levels associated with it, is the number one cause of fish kills- events where many fish die at the same time- in creeks, rivers and streams. Climate change models predict that rising water temperatures are a serious threat to steelhead trout. Adult steelhead thrive at 10-12 degrees Celsius (50-54 degrees Fahrenheit). Temperatures higher than 20 degrees Celsius (68 degrees Fahrenheit) are lethal.

Dissolved Oxygen

All aquatic animal life needs oxygen to survive. Steelhead and the aquatic insects they prey on are especially sensitive to this. Adult steelhead require 7 parts per million (ppm) or higher of dissolved oxygen (DO) in their water to survive. If DO level are 5 ppm or lower this can be lethal. One factor that can decrease the amount of oxygen in the water is higher than average temperatures.

Depth

If there's one thing aquatic organisms need to survive, it's water! Droughts, dams and heavy water use can lower water level in streams, creeks and rivers. We measure depth in centimeters to better understand how seasonal variation, our changing climate and human water use are impacting our watersheds over time.
